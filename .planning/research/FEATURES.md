# FEATURES.md — Portfolio Feature Landscape
*Research for nikkijiang portfolio — Digital Global Health internship application*

## Table Stakes

Features reviewers expect. Missing any of these signals an incomplete portfolio.

| Feature | Why Expected | Complexity |
|---------|--------------|------------|
| Named projects with thumbnail images | Reviewers scan the index in 10–30 seconds; no image = invisible | Low |
| Per-project role and context line | Reviewers ask "what did *you* do?" — especially for research work | Low |
| Problem statement at top of every case study | Need to understand what was being solved before caring about how | Low |
| Process documentation | Evidence of structured thinking, not just polished outputs | Medium |
| Outcomes / results section | Shows completion, not just exploration | Low |
| About page | Reviewers always check this to understand who is behind the work | Low |
| Contact info that is one click away | Failure to make email findable has killed real applications | Low |
| Resume download link | Research orgs expect a document for forwarding to committees | Low |
| Mobile-usable | Reviewers may open on phone after email click | Low |
| Fast load time | Slow portfolio signals poor engineering judgment — doubly bad for a technical role | Medium |

## Differentiators

**1. A clear, stated identity — not just a job title**
"Data storyteller who makes complex health systems legible for non-technical audiences" is a differentiator. "UX Designer" is not.
- Execute: Same framing in home hero, About opening paragraph, and meta description — not paraphrased, the same sentence.

**2. Projects index that feels like a curated body of work, not a dump**
Reviewers decide whether to click based on the gestalt of the index. Write a short framing sentence above the project list; sequence with strongest health work first.
- Execute: 2–3 sentence framing, intentional ordering, Locked Out of Care leads.

**3. Scrollytelling presented as a methodology, not just a project**
Locked Out of Care demonstrates translating dense comparative health data into a navigable narrative — a rare capability. Name the methodology explicitly in the case study.
- Execute: Explain *why* scrollytelling was right for this subject; show design decisions that make data legible (color, annotation, pacing, hierarchy).

**4. Research papers treated as design artifacts, not academic submissions**
Cyberattacks and Laos papers show ability to synthesize complexity, identify a frame, and produce coherent analysis. The case study surfaces that. The PDF is supplementary.
- Execute: See research paper structure below.

**5. Health domain fluency surfaced in writing**
Specific vocabulary signals genuine engagement: "carceral health," "comparative health systems," "digital health infrastructure" — not "healthcare" generically.
- Execute: Use precise terminology throughout case study writing.

**6. Technical range, framed correctly**
Dinner Party and Task Manager show you can build — frame them as proof of execution, not filler.
- Execute: Shorter case studies; lead with what you built and what it does; frame as "technical and creative range."

**7. About page that makes a case, not just a bio**
Most About pages are biographical. Great ones argue why this person fits this role.

## Anti-Features (Avoid These)

| Anti-Feature | Why It Hurts | What to Do Instead |
|--------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Password-protecting the portfolio | Most reviewers won't bother finding credentials | Public only |
| Final deliverables only, no process | Looks like copied work | Show sketches, drafts, one "before/after" |
| "I conducted user research then iterated" with no evidence | Noise without proof | Quote a finding, show a research note |
| Generic mockup frames / Dribbble device templates | Low investment signal | Actual screenshots of the work |
| Long walls of text | Reviewers scan, they don't read | Headers, pull quotes, annotated images |
| Missing "my role" | Reviewer assumes minimal contribution | State role explicitly, even for solo projects |
| Contact form as only contact method | Forms add friction; they also break | Email address directly on the page |
| Skills grid with self-assessment ratings | Reviewers don't trust self-ratings | "I built X using Y" > "Proficient in Y" |
| No description on projects index | Must click to understand anything | 1–2 line description visible on each card |
| Designing for other designers | Health reviewers are not on Dribbble | Prioritize clarity over visual cleverness |
| Burying the health connection | Reviewer assumes generic interest | Health focus in hero, index framing, and About opening |

## How to Present Research Papers

**Section 1 — The question I was answering** (2–3 sentences)
State the research question in plain language — not the abstract, the driving question.

**Section 2 — How I approached it** (1 paragraph + 1–2 images or diagrams)
Describe methodology concretely: what sources, what analytical framework, what structure you imposed on messy information. This is where design thinking shows inside a written document.

**Section 3 — What I found** (3–5 key findings as scannable bullets)
Pull the most important findings out. Reviewer understands your intellectual contribution without reading the full paper.

**Section 4 — Why it matters** (1 paragraph)
Connect findings to real-world stakes — policy, patients, systems.

**Section 5 — Link to full paper** (button or text link)
Label clearly: "Read the full paper — PDF, 24 pages" so reviewers can decide whether to click.

**Do NOT:**
- Embed a PDF iframe as the case study
- Call it a "research paper" on the projects index — call it "Research: Cyberattacks on US Healthcare Infrastructure"

## Case Study Page Structure

### 1. Hero / Header (above the fold)
- Project title
- One-sentence framing: what this is and why it matters
- Key metadata: Role | Context | Year | Tools
- One strong image

### 2. Problem / Context (2–4 sentences)
- What situation or question prompted this work?
- Who is affected and how?

### 3. My Role (2–3 sentences or bullets)
- What did you specifically do?
- What did you own vs. collaborate on?

### 4. Process (core — 3–6 sections with images)
- Approach and why
- Work in progress: sketches, data exploration, early drafts, iterations
- At least one "decision I made and why" moment

### 5. Key Findings / Outcomes
- What did you learn or make?
- For design: final artifact with annotation
- For research papers: 3–5 insights as scannable bullets

### 6. Reflection (optional but differentiating — 2–3 sentences)
- What would you do differently?
- What did this project change in how you think?

### 7. Links
- Live site (if applicable)
- PDF (labeled with page count)
- GitHub (only if code is part of the story)

## About Page Structure

**Opening paragraph — Identity and framing**
Lead with the positioning statement, not a bio. Same framing as home hero — repeated, not varied.

**Why health / global health — 1 paragraph**
Explicitly connect the dots: Locked Out of Care + Laos paper + Cyberattacks = sustained engagement with health systems. Say why global health specifically.

**Background — 2–4 sentences**
Georgetown product design + health background. Brief — the work speaks louder.

**Capabilities — stated as capabilities, not skills grid**
"I work across research, data visualization, and interactive storytelling" > self-rated skill matrix.

**Resume download link** — prominently placed.

**Photo** — Recommended; health organizations are people-centric.

**Avoid:**
- Third person ("Nikki is a designer who...")
- Listing every class taken
- Mission statements that apply to any designer
- Burying the health connection — must appear in the first paragraph
